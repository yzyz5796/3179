Household Income and Poverty in Malaysia

In Malaysia, household income and poverty are vital indicators of economic well-being and social progress. The uneven distribution of wealth, regional disparities, and varying economic opportunities across states significantly impact the standard of living for millions of Malaysians. To better understand these dynamics, I have created several visualizations that explore income levels, poverty rates, and expenditure patterns across the country, aiming to uncover correlations between these factors and highlight areas requiring policy attention for more equitable development.

Median Income by States

From the map above We can observe that concentration of wealth is in more urbanized regions such as states like Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, and Putrajaya which have the highest median incomes. In contrast, states like Kelantan and Perlis have significantly lower median incomes, reflecting regional economic disparities. This visualization helps identify disparities in income distribution across different regions.

Income & Poverty Insights

There is a correlation between income, expenditure, and poverty, as states with lower median incomes, such as Sabah and Kelantan, also show lower levels of average household spending and high poverty rates. This indicates that poorer regions tend to have lower living costs but also less disposable income. These insights can help policymakers address inequalities and identify areas that need the most attention.

Median Income Trend

More urbanized states like Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, and Putrajaya show a steeper increase in median income, especially in recent years, while rural states like Kelantan and Perlis show a slower pace of income growth, highlighting regional income disparities. The gap between high-income and low-income states appears to be widening over time, reflecting uneven economic development across the country.

Relationship between Income and Expenditure

States with higher median incomes, such as Selangor and Putrajaya, are clustered at the upper end of the graph with larger bubbles, indicating better spending ability. The growth of these bubbles over time suggests that the disparity between wealthier and less affluent regions is increasing.
